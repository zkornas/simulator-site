My Research

Bandwidth Inflation Attacks in Tor

I am currently working on a research project exploring bandwidth inflation attacks within Tor. Specifically, we are researching how Tor’s bandwidth scanner (sbws) is used to verify bandwidth measurements of relays within the Tor network, and how an adversary can manipulate these readings to gain an unfair advantage in circuit construction. To do this, we have constructed a simulated Tor network using Raspberry Pis acting as various nodes. We hope to survey the current literature around bandwidth inflation attacks, understand how sbws currently attempts to mitigate bandwidth inflation attacks, and propose a solution to this issue that can work alongside or replace Tor’s current bandwidth scanner.

SimulaTor

While tools such as Chutney and Shadow allow researchers to create virtual Tor networks, there is no resource to help researchers create a simulated Tor network using physical nodes. While researching bandwidth inflation attacks, I have been compiling information on creating a physical private Tor network and plan to release the SimulaTor framework soon. Replicability is extremely important in research, and I hope that the SimulaTor framework will allow other researchers to be able to easily create simulated Tor networks to verify others' findings.

My Projects

Oops! I Hacked My Professor >:)

The professor for our cybersecurity course challenged all students to try to hack him. Using a combination of social engineering and technical aptitude, I was the only student in the course to successfully plan and execute an attack against my professor.

Through my reconnaissance, I was able to determine the best avenue to carry out my attack. I decided to conduct a phishing attack by sending my target a link to a malicious web page I was hosting. The webpage redirected to a clone of my university's SSO page, and I could potentially save the login information entered by my target to perform future attacks. The professor did try to login with their school login, thus making my phishing attack a success.

This project taught me the importance of social engineering as well as technical competence needed for an adversary to carry out a successful attack.

Tic-Tac-Toe

In this project, I worked with a team to develop a Java-based multiplayer Tic-Tac-Toe game following the provided Tic-Tac-Toe RFC. Utilizing a server-client architecture, players were able to connect seamlessly from multiple platforms to host and play games while ensuring universal compatibility. We were able to implement many user-friendly features, such as game session management, real-time move updates, and game result notifications. Check out our Tic-Tac-Toe game here!

DubDumps

DubDumps was a website designed to help students at the University of Washington locate the nearest restroom. We integrated useful features for students such as bathroom rating systems, structured searches, and ability to login with student email. Students are able to use DubDumps to locate bathrooms that meet their requirements, such as accessibility, proximity, and amenities offered in each restroom.

“The goal of DubDumps is to support our current community’s public health, promote personal hygiene, and ultimately make our campus a more accessible place.”
